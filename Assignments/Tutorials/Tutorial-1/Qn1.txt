Procedure-Oriented Programming  is best for small programs, and systems with limited hardware memory. In POP, the main focus is on writing functions or a list of steps to solve a problem. It works from the top down, meaning the program just follows a sequence of instructions. This approach is perfect for things like operating systems, microchips in home appliances, or quick mathematical calculators. Because it does not have complex structures, it runs incredibly fast and gives you direct control over the computer's hardware.

Object-Oriented Programming  is needed when building large, complex software like mobile apps, video games, or banking systems. OOP works by breaking a big program down into smaller, real-world pieces called "objects." For example, a banking app will have objects like a Customer and a BankAccount. Each object safely locks its own data inside so other parts of the program cannot accidentally change or ruin it. It also us to reuse old code easily, which saves a lot of time when teams of developers work together on the same project for years.

In short, we should choose POP when your program is simple and needs to run as fast as possible on the hardware. We should choose OOP when our project is large, has many moving parts, and needs to be easily updated or expanded in the future without breaking.
